## What This Paper Is

A short field-research article in a feminist environmental-studies magazine reporting on a participatory qualitative study of urbanization and tourism pressures in the caiçara fishing community of Picinguaba, Brazil. The piece compresses the research project's empirical findings into a narrative for a non-academic audience and frames the resulting governance challenges in feminist-economy terms: it foregrounds the leadership of women in local decision-making and argues for community-based tourism over privatization.

## Research Question

Implicit central question: *How are urbanization and the rapid expansion of social-media-driven tourism reshaping livelihoods, governance, and ecological wellbeing in Picinguaba, and what governance pathways could the community take?*

Subquestions visible in the piece:

- How has tourism altered the income distribution and the relative profits of boatmen vs. land-based businesses?
- What are the cultural and intergenerational consequences of the livelihood shift from fishing / agriculture / hunting to tourism?
- How does increased tourism affect marine and coastal conservation (over-fishing, sewage, fuel, garbage)?
- Why have women come to the fore in Picinguaba's tourism governance when this is uncommon in comparable Brazilian fishing communities?
- What scenarios are realistically available to the community — community-based tourism, profit-driven tourism, or privatization of Couves Island?
- What governance arrangement (including external facilitation) could help residents converge on a path?

## Motivation

The authors situate the piece in a broader concern about how rapidly urbanizing coastal areas in the Global South can absorb tourism without losing the cultural diversity and small-scale livelihoods that make them attractive in the first place. They frame Picinguaba as a "living laboratory" for understanding urbanization processes and community-based coastal governance — a setting where three generations of culturally diverse families have lived through a transition from subsistence to market-mediated tourism in a few decades.

The magazine's themed issue celebrates "economies of change" and inclusive futures, so the piece is also a deliberate contribution to a feminist-economic argument: women's leadership in Picinguaba's negotiations is treated not as ornamental but as the local mechanism through which a less-extractive tourism economy might be built.

## Setting / Data / Context

**Place.** Picinguaba is a Brazilian coastal community on the southeast coast in the town of Ubatuba, São Paulo State. It sits at the foot of preserved coastal mountains, with fragments of Atlantic forest reaching the sea. It lies within or adjacent to the *Serra do Mar State Park*, a protected area covering both land and marine zones around Picinguaba.

**Caiçara identity.** "Caiçara" denotes a mixed cultural identity composed of descendants of European immigrants, Africans, and Indigenous peoples, whose livelihoods historically rested on small-scale agriculture, fishing, and other local jobs. Most of the region's marginalized fishing communities have a tradition of participatory governance.

**Pre-tourism economy (up to ~1980s).** Fishing, hunting, and agriculture sustained families. Fishers went to sea daily for mullet and other seafood. Families grew fruits and vegetables, hunted small mammals and birds in the Atlantic forest, and exchanged products among themselves. The piece reproduces a direct quote from an elderly resident: *"We did not see money, but we did not lack food! We used to exchange cassava flour for fish."*

**Trigger of urbanization.** A federal highway built in the 1970s connected the Brazilian coast from north to south, gradually integrating Picinguaba into a tourism economy.

**Tourism trajectory.** Through the early 2010s, tourism developed as a complementary source of income — fishers offered boat trips to *Couves Island* (Ilha das Couves). Locals broadly perceived the increase as positive. From approximately 2017, social-media advertisements branding Couves Island as a "paradise on Earth" drove a sharp spike in tourist volume. Picinguaba became "little more than an access point to the island."

**Distributional effect of the spike.** Boatmen's profits rose. Profits of land-based businesses — restaurants, hotels, markets — fell. Illegal parking disrupted local transport. Fuel from boats and garbage left by tourists polluted waters; turbidity rose and aesthetic value declined. Conflicts emerged between boatmen wanting more tourism and other residents wanting community-based tourism with limits on island visitors.

**Concurrent governance pressure.** Because of environmental degradation, the local protected area and the government began discussing privatizing Couves Island. The Serra do Mar State Park's zoning plan, published in 2008, does not consider the environmental and socioeconomic pressures that emerged after 2017.

**Gender configuration.** Women are centrally involved in local management — for example, taking the lead in the local business owners' association and participating in negotiations about protected areas. The authors note this is "not as common in other Brazilian communities, where those who participate in such negotiations are frequently men."

## Methods / Model

Qualitative, participatory research:

- **Narratives** collected across three age cohorts:
  - *Seniors,* aged 60 and over.
  - *Adults,* aged 40 to 60.
  - *Young people,* aged 20 to 40.
- **35 interviews** in total.
- **Participatory workshop** in which community members discussed the situation themselves.

Narratives focused on:

- Changes in local livelihoods.
- Use and status of ecosystem services.
- Wellbeing of each generation.

The workshop centered on how social–ecological changes interfere with community wellbeing. Together these methods produced a narrative account of changes experienced in Picinguaba and their impacts on community members' wellbeing.

The article does not present coding categories, sample selection criteria, interview-guide content, or formal qualitative-analysis steps — those would presumably appear in the underlying academic outputs of the same project. The piece is a magazine summary, not the full research write-up.

## Key Variables or Constructs

- **Caiçara cultural identity** — mixed European, African, Indigenous descent; small-scale agriculture and fishing; participatory governance traditions.
- **Trade-offs among economic revenue, local tradition, and environmental conservation** — the central framing of community deliberation.
- **Tourism modes:** community-based (oriented to communal profit + cultural appreciation), profit-driven (current trajectory), and privatized (sale of the island to outside management).
- **Ecosystem services** — coastal and forest amenities used both subsistently (food, materials) and commercially (beach aesthetic, marine biodiversity).
- **Material, relational, and subjective wellbeing** — the article's wellbeing framing distinguishes these three dimensions of community impact.
- **Three generations** — seniors / adults / young people, as both interview cohorts and as social positions experiencing distinct livelihood transitions.
- **Women's leadership in governance** — both an empirical finding about Picinguaba's specificity and a normative anchor of the piece's policy proposal.
- **External "third party" facilitation** — a community consultant or neutral governmental agency proposed as a vehicle to broker community-based tourism.

## Results

The piece does not present quantitative results. Its empirical content is a set of narrative findings about how community life has changed and where the conflict lines fall.

### How tourism reshaped Picinguaba's economy

- Initial integration of tourism into local livelihoods was complementary and positively perceived. Tourism happens mostly in spring/summer; small-scale agriculture and fishing dominate in winter.
- Tourism brought new employment opportunities, new markets for local products, opportunities for cultural appreciation (e.g., local food in restaurants), and a path to reduce out-migration to nearby cities.
- After the 2017 social-media-driven tourism spike, the distributive effect reversed: boatmen owning speedboats gained competitive advantage and rising income; restaurant, hotel, and market profits declined.

### How tourism reshaped social cohesion

- The flow of people in and out of the community has weakened community trust.
- Drug use has gained ground.
- Young people focus on tourism rather than fishing, reducing intergenerational transmission of fishing knowledge and the social relations sustained through fishing.
- Threats to social cohesion have detrimental consequences for local traditions and culture.

### How tourism reshaped the environment

- Increased tourism income enabled the purchase of new fishing gear, raising fishers' capacity and creating pressure for over-fishing in response to tourist demand for fish.
- Extractive and visitor activities increased habitat degradation: sewage, garbage, and fuel pollution from speedboats running to Couves Island.
- Environmental degradation reduces the aesthetic value of Picinguaba's beaches. The piece reproduces a direct quote from a young woman: *"When I want to enjoy the beach, I don't come here anymore. I have to go to other beaches."*

### Two (effectively three) scenarios the community faces

1. **Community-based tourism.** Restaurant and hotel owners and boatmen jointly develop a tourism plan and reach an agreement on community-based tourism. Tourism focuses on communal profit in association with appreciation of local culture.
2. **Privatization of Couves Island.** Outsiders purchase the right to manage the area and regulate boat trips, regardless of community collaboration. The community loses its stake in decisions about tourism on the Island and would have to adapt to sharing local beaches with tourists, possibly with the prospect of some jobs on the Island.

The current profit-driven, fragmented mode is treated implicitly as a third de-facto scenario — the trajectory the community is on if no governance intervention occurs.

### Governance challenges named in the article

- **Outdated zoning.** The Serra do Mar State Park zoning plan (2008) does not consider the environmental and socioeconomic issues that emerged with the post-2017 tourism spike.
- **Cultural valuation gap.** Some groups in the community have a strong focus on individual profit-oriented tourism, while others maintain small-scale fishing, agriculture, and hunting as important. Communication between these groups is poor and conflicts are escalating, making community-based tourism difficult to implement.

### The authors' proposal

An outside "third party" — e.g., a community consultant or neutral governmental agency — might play a useful role in helping elucidate options, develop communication pathways, and facilitate agreement about the desired future for local tourism. Because many of the local leaders are women, the situation in Picinguaba may offer a unique opportunity to foster gender balance and empower women's participation in local governance initiatives. The authors emphasize this is necessarily a long-term process requiring building trust among local parties and including negotiations with government and regulatory agencies related to the protected areas.

## Contributions

- Documents a specific *post-social-media* turning point (~2017) in a Latin American coastal-tourism trajectory, distinguishing the gradual integration phase from the rupture phase.
- Reports the *distributive reversal* of tourism gains as a function of who owns boats vs. who runs land-based businesses — a finding that complicates aggregate "tourism is good for income" narratives.
- Identifies an *empirical anomaly* — Picinguaba's women-led governance of protected-area negotiations — and reads it as a leverage point for gender-balanced coastal governance rather than as a curiosity.
- Distills a participatory research project (35 interviews across three age cohorts plus a workshop) into a policy-readable scenario framing.
- Makes a concrete *governance proposal*: third-party facilitation rather than either pure market expansion or top-down protected-area enforcement.

## Limitations and Assumptions

The piece is a magazine summary and does not present its own methodological limitations explicitly. Implicit limitations a future reader should keep in mind:

- *Scope.* The findings come from one community (Picinguaba) at one historical moment. The authors do not claim external validity for other Brazilian coastal communities or globally.
- *Sample.* 35 interviews + one workshop is appropriate for the qualitative goal of recovering narratives across three generations, but it is not designed to estimate population-level effects.
- *No quantification.* The economic claims (boatmen's profits rose, land-based business profits fell, over-fishing pressure increased) are reported as qualitative findings from interviews, not from economic data.
- *No counterfactual.* The piece does not estimate what Picinguaba's trajectory would have looked like without the social-media-driven tourism spike, or without women's leadership.
- *No comparative case.* The claim that women's leadership is uncommon in comparable Brazilian fishing communities is stated as background knowledge rather than demonstrated within the piece.
- *Source-of-truth framing.* The choice to organize findings around "community wellbeing" rests on the authors' interpretive framework; alternative framings (purely environmental, purely economic) would yield different emphases.

Embedded assumptions:

- A community-based tourism arrangement is feasible if a credible third-party facilitator is available.
- Gender balance in coastal-zone governance is itself a desirable policy outcome, not just instrumentally valuable for community-based tourism.
- The dichotomy "community-based vs. privatized Couves Island" is the relevant policy choice — i.e., a hybrid (partial privatization, partial community management) is not separately considered.

## What the Paper Does Not Do

- Does not present the full qualitative research project — interview protocols, coding scheme, transcripts, or analytic memos are not included.
- Does not propose a specific institutional design for the third-party facilitator (who selects it, how it is funded, what authority it has).
- Does not quantify the environmental damage (e.g., water quality measurements, fish stock changes, garbage / sewage volumes).
- Does not provide income or wage data for boatmen vs. other community members.
- Does not engage formally with the academic literatures on common-pool resource governance, community-based natural resource management, or tourism economics — though Picinguaba's situation closely tracks themes in those literatures.
- Does not compare Picinguaba with other Brazilian or Latin American cases where similar tourism transitions have happened.
- Does not address what would happen to caiçara cultural identity under either scenario in a more than narrative sense.
- Does not test whether the 2008 Serra do Mar zoning plan could be updated without privatization.

## Key Takeaways

- A post-2017 social-media-driven tourism spike turned Picinguaba into an access point for Couves Island, reversing the distribution of gains away from land-based businesses toward boat owners and amplifying environmental degradation.
- The piece documents Picinguaba's unusual configuration of women-led tourism governance and treats it as both an empirical finding and a leverage point.
- The community faces a fork between community-based tourism (requiring agreement among boatmen, restaurants, and hotels) and privatization of Couves Island (which removes community decision-rights). The status-quo trajectory is implicitly a third option that erodes both livelihoods and conservation.
- The authors recommend external "third-party" facilitation by a community consultant or neutral governmental agency, paired with deliberate support for women's leadership.
- Picinguaba is framed as a "living laboratory" for community-based coastal governance — a generalizable site for studying urbanization–tourism–gender–conservation interactions rather than only a Brazilian local case.
