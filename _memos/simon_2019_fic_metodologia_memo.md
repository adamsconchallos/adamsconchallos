## What This Paper Is

A methodological pre-report documenting the Mamdani-type fuzzy-logic model used to integrate tacit and explicit knowledge for a regional public policy on sustainable aquaculture in the Biobío region of Chile. The report covers (i) a theoretical introduction to fuzzy logic, (ii) a review of fuzzy-logic applications in aquaculture and ecosystem management, (iii) a three-stage methodology spanning knowledge elicitation through fuzzy-model construction to strategic prioritization, and (iv) three annexes (press review 2013–2018; survey instrument; glossary of critical variables). Produced by INCAR / Universidad de Concepción under FIC-R 2017 project FIC 4000116.

## Research Question

How can tacit (stakeholder) and explicit (scientific / regulatory / sectoral) knowledge be jointly modelled to identify and rank strategic interventions for sustainable aquaculture in a region (Biobío) where information is fragmented, incomplete, and partly conflicting?

Subquestions:

- Which actors hold relevant tacit knowledge and how should they be mapped?
- How can heterogeneous variables (ecological, socioeconomic, governance) be integrated when some are quantitative and others are qualitative expert judgements?
- How should membership functions and fuzzy rules be elicited to reflect both expert opinion and stakeholder perception?
- How should outputs of a Mamdani-type model be interpreted as sustainability indices?
- Which variables are sufficiently sensitive to drive strategic priorities and project profiles?

## Motivation

Regional aquaculture policy in Chile has historically been designed using national instruments and quantitative models (e.g., MOM; Stigebrandt 2011; Quiñones et al. 2019). According to the authors, this approach (i) under-uses local tacit knowledge of producers, communities, and territorial managers, and (ii) handles uncertainty by exclusion (incomplete cases dropped) rather than by representation. Fuzzy logic — developed by Zadeh (1965) and applied to ecosystems by Silvert (2000), Marchini (2011), and others — offers a way to integrate fragmented, qualitative, and uncertain information into a transparent model whose outputs can be read both quantitatively (sustainability scores 0–100) and linguistically (e.g., "aceptable").

The authors note that, to their knowledge, fuzzy logic has not previously been used in Chile for aquaculture-related decision tools, with the partial exceptions of Meza & Vargas (2009) on native-fish habitat quality in the Huequecura river and Yáñez et al. (2004) on anchovy fishing areas in northern Chile.

## Setting / Data / Context

**Geography.** Biobío and Ñuble regions of southern Chile. The methodology was designed to support a regional public-policy proposal documented separately in the Estudio Base.

**Knowledge inputs.**

- *Explicit:* scientific literature on aquaculture and ecosystems; technical reports (SERNAPESCA, SUBPESCA, FIPA, FAO); regulatory texts (LGPA, RAMA, regional and national policies); population and labour data (INE, CONADI); water-rights data (DGA); environmental-assessment records (SEA).
- *Tacit:* semi-structured interviews + closed-form surveys with informants from public and private sectors, productive-chain links, and local / provincial / regional territories. A press review for 2013–2018 (Anexo I) supported actor identification and conflict mapping.

**Survey instrument.** 59 variables: 24 ecological, 24 socioeconomic, 11 governance. Variables grouped under the FAO Ecosystem Approach to Aquaculture (EEA / EAA) — the ecological dimension was further split into "ecosystem quality" and "effects produced by aquaculture on the ecosystem" (FAO 2010). Interviews were recorded and transcribed; survey responses were digitized.

## Methods / Model

### Fuzzy logic primer (Section 2)

Fuzzy logic represents linguistic categories (e.g., "agua aceptable" vs. "agua no aceptable") as overlapping fuzzy sets defined by *membership functions* μ ∈ [0, 1]. A value of 50 on a water-quality scale of 1–100, for example, can simultaneously belong 0.22 to "Aceptable" and 0.78 to "No Aceptable" — the intersection zone (41–69 in the worked example) represents evaluator uncertainty rather than forcing a binary choice. Outside the intersection (1–40 and 70–100), membership is 0 in one set and 1 in the other.

This contrasts with classical binary logic by allowing *partial truth* and is well-suited to fragmented, qualitative, or uncertain data (Klir & Yuan 1995; Sivanandam et al. 2007; Marchini 2011).

### Three-stage methodology (Section 3)

**Stage 1 — Knowledge collection and systematization (tacit + explicit).**

1. Establish theoretical framework: FAO Ecosystem Approach to Aquaculture (EEA), three dimensions (ecological / socioeconomic / governance); study area = Biobío + Ñuble.
2. Stakeholder map: identify interested parties (those affected by or affecting decisions), decision-makers (those with authority), and experts (those with relevant knowledge), following Reed (2008) and Días et al. (2017). Three phases: (a) define system boundaries from theoretical framework; (b) preliminary identification via secondary sources including a 2013–2018 press review (Anexo I), the 2016 Mapa de Actores for a regional aquaculture policy, and team tacit knowledge; (c) validation interviews + Social Network Analysis (Reed et al. 2009; Prell et al. 2009; Ackermann & Eden 2011; Ahmadi et al. 2019) combined with stakeholder-analysis methods (Mitchell et al. 1997; Chevalier & Buckles 2008).
3. Explicit-knowledge collection: scientific literature, R&D project search by keywords (Spanish + English), technical reports from SERNAPESCA / SUBPESCA / FIPA, statistical data, transparency-law requests (Ley 20.285), INE / CONADI population and labour data, DGA water-rights data, SEA environmental assessments, regulatory and normative review.
4. Survey-instrument construction: section 1 semi-structured interview on actors and gaps/potentials; section 2 closed-form survey on the main variables from explicit knowledge, aligned with the EEA dimensions; ecological dimension split into "ecosystem quality" and "effects of aquaculture on the ecosystem."
5. Tacit-knowledge collection: purposive sampling + theoretical-saturation criterion (Briones 2002; Corbin & Strauss 2008; Hernández et al. 2010; Taylor et al. 2016). Sample emphasis on variety across sectors (public / private / productive chain) and territories (local / provincial / regional). Interviews recorded and transcribed; gaps and potentials systematized into the diagnostic.

**Stage 2 — Mamdani-type fuzzy logic model construction.**

a. *Fuzzification.* Each of the 59 input variables is associated with membership functions describing its linguistic categories. Tacit-knowledge survey responses (1–100 scales) and explicit-knowledge values are mapped onto these membership functions.

b. *Inference.* Fuzzy if-then rules connect input variables to output categories. Worked example: "If water quality is good AND egg quality is bad, then the ecological index is acceptable (70–80)." Membership-function shapes can be triangular, trapezoidal, sigmoidal; the number of categories depends on the level of uncertainty about each variable. Each rule produces a partial output (μ ∈ [0, 1]).

c. *Defuzzification.* Partial outputs are aggregated using the *centre-of-gravity* (COG) method (Sivanandam et al. 2007) implemented in MATLAB, yielding a single sustainability index per dimension (range 0–100) plus a linguistic label (e.g., "bueno", "aceptable"). MATLAB visualizes interactions among two input variables with the sustainability index as the Z-axis.

Sensitivity analysis on the 59 input variables reduces the working set to 22 critical variables for which small changes in input produce marked changes in the sustainability index. Annex III contains a glossary of those critical variables.

Complementary membership functions are also built using explicit knowledge only, and these interact with the tacit-knowledge functions in the inference step.

**Stage 3 — Prioritization and project profiles.**

- Sensitive (high-leverage) variables identified from model outputs are used to build a project-profile portfolio (cartera de perfiles de proyectos).
- Strategic axes are then derived from the variable clusters, and policy-instrument proposals are mapped to each axis.
- The Estudio Base reports the four resulting strategic axes and their critical-variable targets.

## Key Variables or Constructs

- **Membership function (μ).** Maps a quantitative value (1–100) to a degree of belonging in a fuzzy set (e.g., "aceptable" or "no aceptable"). Shape can be triangular, trapezoidal, sigmoidal; number of sets reflects uncertainty.
- **Linguistic variable.** A variable whose values are words (e.g., "good", "acceptable", "poor") rather than numbers.
- **Partial truth (degree of truth).** A value between 0 and 1 representing how true a proposition is, rather than the binary {0, 1}.
- **Fuzzy if-then rule.** Implication linking input membership values to an output category.
- **Defuzzification (COG).** Aggregates partial outputs into a single numeric score using centre-of-gravity.
- **Sustainability index.** Numeric score (0–100) per EEA dimension (ecological / socioeconomic / governance), accompanied by a linguistic label.
- **Critical / sensitive variable.** A variable whose small change shifts the dimension's sustainability index materially; identified by sensitivity analysis.
- **EEA dimensions.** Ecological (split into ecosystem quality + effects from aquaculture), socioeconomic, governance.

## Equations (paraphrased symbolically; exact prose definitions are in the source)

- Fuzzy set A on universe X has membership function μ_A : X → [0, 1], where μ_A(x) is the degree to which x belongs to A.
- For two overlapping sets A and B on the same universe, a value x can have μ_A(x) > 0 and μ_B(x) > 0 simultaneously, and the intersection region is the locus of evaluator uncertainty.
- A Mamdani-type rule has the form: IF (x1 is A1) AND (x2 is A2) AND … THEN (y is B), where Ai and B are fuzzy sets defined by membership functions.
- Partial outputs from each rule are aggregated into a single fuzzy output set; defuzzification yields a crisp value via the centre-of-gravity:

```
y* = ∫ y · μ_out(y) dy  /  ∫ μ_out(y) dy
```

(Paraphrased symbolically; the source does not present the formula in this form but describes the method by name, following Sivanandam et al. 2007 and Mathworks 2013.)

## Results

This is a methodology pre-report rather than a results-paper. The "results" are the operational design choices:

- **22 critical variables** retained from the 59 input variables after sensitivity analysis (full glossary in Anexo III; the main text gives worked examples).
- **Three sustainability indices** generated per EEA dimension (ecological, governance, socioeconomic).
- **Worked example for the socioeconomic dimension** (Figure 3 in the source): when "Nivel produccion contratada = 70, tiempo de recuperacion inversión = 70, nivel de empleo = 20," the maximum achievable sustainability index is 75. The figure plots "nivel de ingresos generados por acuicultura" and "Acceso a financiamiento" on the X and Y axes with the sustainability index on Z.
- **Three strategic axes feeding into the Estudio Base policy matrix** (the report explicitly hands off to the Estudio Base for the substantive matrix; the methodology produces the design of the matrix, not its content).

## Robustness / Validity / Sensitivity Checks

- Variable reduction from 59 → 22 via sensitivity analysis: variables that fail to move the dimension index materially under small input changes are dropped.
- Complementary explicit-only membership functions are constructed to interact with the tacit-knowledge functions in the inference step — partial cross-validation of tacit-vs-explicit emphasis.
- Theoretical-saturation criterion in tacit-knowledge sampling: interviews continued until additional informants produced no new themes (Briones 2002; Corbin & Strauss 2008).
- Press review (Anexo I) for 2013–2018 cross-checks the actor map against publicly reported conflicts.
- Mamdani-type was chosen for its capacity to deliver simultaneously quantitative and qualitative outputs (Shepard 2005; Marchini 2011; Nassif et al. 2013; Mordeson et al. 2015).

## Contributions

- First (per authors' knowledge) Chilean application of fuzzy logic to aquaculture decision-support.
- Methodological pre-report that decouples the *method* (fuzzy logic on EEA dimensions) from its *substantive application* (the Biobío policy proposal in the Estudio Base) — supporting reuse in other regions.
- Concrete protocol for integrating stakeholder tacit knowledge with explicit regulatory and scientific knowledge via membership functions and fuzzy if-then rules.
- Documentation of the survey instrument (Anexo II), the critical-variable glossary (Anexo III), and the press-review evidence base (Anexo I).

## Limitations and Assumptions

Explicit limitations:

- Subjectivity of membership-function shape and rule definitions: these reflect expert + stakeholder judgement and would change with a different respondent pool.
- The selection of categorical thresholds (e.g., "aceptable" range 41–69 in the worked example) is paramaterized rather than derived.
- Theoretical saturation is the stopping criterion for tacit-knowledge sampling, not statistical representativeness.
- The MATLAB implementation produces 3-D visualization across only two input variables at a time; higher-order interactions among the 22 critical variables are not directly visualized.

Embedded assumptions:

- Tacit and explicit knowledge can be brought onto a common scale via membership functions.
- The EEA's three dimensions (with the ecological dimension's two sub-dimensions) are an adequate partition of the policy-relevant variable space.
- A Mamdani-type model is appropriate for the structure of the data; other fuzzy-model families (Sugeno, Tsukamoto) are mentioned only generically.
- Centre-of-gravity is the appropriate defuzzification method; alternatives are not benchmarked.

## What the Paper Does Not Do

- Does not present the substantive policy matrix or the four strategic axes — those live in the Estudio Base.
- Does not formally compare fuzzy-logic outputs against an alternative such as multi-criteria decision analysis or a Bayesian belief network.
- Does not provide the numerical inputs (survey response distributions) used to fit the membership functions in the main text — those are summarized but not tabulated.
- Does not specify a uniform membership-function shape: rule-by-rule selection is described in general terms.
- Does not formally validate the model against an out-of-sample dataset.
- Does not implement the methodology in any region other than Biobío + Ñuble.

## Key Takeaways

- The fuzzy-logic methodology integrates tacit and explicit knowledge through membership functions and Mamdani if-then rules, with a centre-of-gravity defuzzification yielding sustainability indices per EEA dimension.
- The survey instrument captures 59 variables (24 ecological, 24 socioeconomic, 11 governance), reduced to 22 critical variables via sensitivity analysis.
- The methodology is the engine behind the four strategic axes and the logical-framework matrix presented in the companion Estudio Base.
- The approach is explicitly portable: the report frames its method as transferable to other regions facing similar issues of fragmented and partly tacit information.
